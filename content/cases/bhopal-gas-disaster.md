The Bhopal gas disaster is now framed as an industrial-disaster case rather than a generic tragedy placeholder. The strongest public record is clear on the event identity: a methyl isocyanate leak from the Union Carbide plant in Bhopal on 3 December 1984, with long-term casualties counted in the tens of thousands and survivors affected for decades. A May 2026 Outlook India report added that survivors were still battling collapsing health-care systems, delayed approvals, missing specialists and exhausting bureaucracy, with one patient waiting for the remaining Rs 2 lakh after a partially sanctioned kidney-transplant aid request.

The accountability picture remains incomplete. BBC's 2024 retrospective says an Indian court convicted seven former managers in 2010, while BBC's 2023 reporting on the compensation ruling says the Supreme Court rejected the government's plea for more money and Dow maintained the 1989 settlement was fair and final.

In 2025, the entire 337 tonnes of Union Carbide waste were incinerated in Pithampur, the CBI opposed retrial pleas by three former company officials in September, the Madhya Pradesh High Court ordered district courts to expedite the remaining criminal revision appeals and send monthly reports, and later that year the court again pressed the state to move the residual ash containment site away from human habitation after warning that the ash was still toxic and that mercury levels remained above permissible limits. In March 2026, the High Court directed the state government to submit a five-week assessment-and-remediation plan for contaminated soil and groundwater at the former UCIL site after an action-taken report said officials had met to discuss time-bound remediation; in May 2026, an Economic Times report said the Supreme Court rejected the Centre's plea seeking additional compensation for victims; in May 2026, Outlook India described the ongoing survivor-care burden in Bhopal's health system; and in June 2026 a Times of India report said a Supreme Court panel told the government to bear the full cost of treatment for Bhopal gas survivors.
